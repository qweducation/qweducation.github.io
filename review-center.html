Answer:

It is possible that an executive order directing the Commission on Higher Education (CHED) to regulate the operation of review centers could be a valid exercise of police power. Police power is the inherent power of the state to regulate the affairs of its citizens in order to promote the general welfare and to protect the health, safety, and morals of the community. It is often used to regulate activities that may pose a potential harm to the public.

If the operation of review centers is seen as having the potential to harm the public, such as by providing inadequate or misleading information to students preparing for exams, then the government may have the authority to regulate their operation through the exercise of police power. However, the validity of such an executive order would depend on the specific circumstances and the legal and constitutional framework of the country in which it is issued.

EXPLANATIONS:1.DISECT

Grammar issue presented: Parallelism (correlative pairs)

Grammar issue presented: Parallelism (correlative pairs)This sentence uses the correlative pair “Both…and” to present two characteristics. Both characteristics should be in the same form, but one is a possessive pronoun + noun (his determination), while the other is a phrase (the way he paid attention to detail). We need another possessive pronoun + noun: “his attention to detail.”

ANALYZE

"and his attention to detail"

Is it parallel? YES – his determination (pronoun + noun) / his attention to detail (pronoun + noun)

Is it parallel? YES – his determination (pronoun + noun) / his attention to detail (pronoun + noun)Additional errors? NONE

2.DISECT

Grammar issue presented: Parallelism (lists of adjectives)

Grammar issue presented: Parallelism (lists of adjectives)This sentence presents a list of qualities. The first two are adjectives (“spacious” and “pleasantly cluttered”), while the third is a verb phrase (“has good lighting”). The third quality must also be an adjective.

ANALYZE

"and well-lit"

Is it parallel? YES – spacious, pleasantly cluttered (adjectives) : well-lit (adjective)

Is it parallel? YES – spacious, pleasantly cluttered (adjectives) : well-lit (adjective)Additional errors? NONE

3.DISECT

Grammar issue presented: Parallelism (lists of verbs)

Grammar issue presented: Parallelism (lists of verbs)This sentence presents a list of two actions. The first verb, “be obtained,” is in the passive voice and is governed by the idiom “request that x be y” The second verb must also be in the form (be) reviewed.

Below are the answers to the questions dealing with parallel structure

Parallel structure, or is also commonly referred to parallelism is a type of structure in English which holds that the words used in a sentence will be having the same pattern with the same degree of importance among each other. For example, Exercising will help you to build your muscle, maintain your stamina, and a better fat burning process.

In a glance, the example above does not seem to have any grammatical error, but, the phrases do not stand in the same ground. "Build your muscle", and "maintain your stamina" are considered equal, because the structure is the same (Verb + adjective), whereas "a better fat burning process" as a structure is adjective + verb. Therefore, that sentence is not a parallel structure.

So, what is the correct example?

1. D. and his attention to detail, as the previous phrase goes like "his determination", so the answer should follow the same pattern, hence his attention to detail.

2. D. and well list, because the previous pattern only mentions adjective: spacious, pleasantly cluttered.

3. the answer is the residency requirements reviewed, because the former pattern is "noun + to be + verb". So, the correct answer should be C.

4. B. and constructing a new varsity athletic center. The previous two patterns follow verb + noun, so the third pattern should be verb + noun, too.

5. D. Richard is not only a terrific pianist, but also a great hockey player. The remaining four choices are wrong because they don't follow the same pattern of adjective + noun (terrific pianist), so for the sentence to be well-balanced, the answer should be "a great hockey player" (adjective + noun).
