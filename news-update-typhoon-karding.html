12. hiw did the sierra madre mountain ranges protect us from super typhoon karding?​


Karding, internationally named Noru, was expected to be a super typhoon as strong as Odette—a storm that wreaked havoc in Visayas and Mindanao. While the storm ripped through Luzon, it was downgraded to a typhoon thanks to the Philippines' shield against turbulent weather: the Sierra Madre.

The super typhoon touted maximum sustained wind speeds of 195 km/h on Sept. 25, with PAGASA raising alerts to Signal No. 5 in Quezon, Pampanga, Bulacan, and Rizal. In Metro Manila and Nueva Ecija, heavy rainfall warning was issued in anticipation of the storm. But as Karding hit the Sierra Madre, the country's longest mountain range, satellite images showed how its rainband started to shrink.

"When the storm passed the Sierra Madre in the Nueva Ecija area, parang lumiit yung [rainband] kung saan naka-concentrate yung malakas na pag-ulan," ABS-CBN meteorologist Ariel Rojas said in a TeleRadyo interview.

25. essay about typhoon karding 150 words​


Tropical storm Noru (also known as Karding locally) intensified dramatically on September 25 and is now a super typhoon as it moves near the Philippines' eastern seaboard. It represents one of the Pacific basin's fastest-ever rapid intensifications. The eyewall of Super Typhoon Noru, which has maximum sustained winds of 195 km/h and gustiness of up to 240 km/h, is currently impacting the Polillo Islands, according to PAGASA, the weather bureau for the Philippines (10-min interval). Later tonight, it is anticipated to make landfall in Quezon Province or Aurora Province in central Luzon's densely populated area.

According to PAGASA, it is also becoming more likely that the storm would maintain typhoon strength once it crosses Luzon's mainland. The highest possible Tropical Cyclone Wind Signal (TCWS) No. 5 (185 km/h or more) has been issued over parts of the Polillo Islands in the provinces of Quezon, Bulacan, and Pampanga, and there is a significant risk of storm surge in the exposed and low-lying coastal areas. Marikina, Caloocan, Malabon, Navotas, Valenzuela, and Quezon City are among the areas of Metro Manila that are currently subject to TCWS No. 4 (118-184 km/h), while the remainder of the area is under TCWS No. 3 (89-117 km/h).
