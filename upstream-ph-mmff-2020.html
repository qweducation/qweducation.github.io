Step-by-step explanation:

x = boat's speed

from the formula

d = st

d is distance

s is speed

t is time

t = d/s

upstream: (the boat is moving against the current)

t1 = 15/(x - 2)

downstream: (the boat is moving along with the current)

t2 = 15/(x + 2)

We know that the total travel time is 3 hours

t1 + t2 = 3

[15/(x - 2)] + [15/(x + 2)] = 3

15(x + 2) + 15(x - 2) = 3(x² - 4)

15x + 30 + 15x - 30 = 3x² - 12

30x = 3x² - 12

3x² - 30x - 12 = 0

x² - 10x - 4 = 0 (this quadratic equation cannot be factored, use quadratic formula)

x = 5 + √29 or 5 - √29

convert to decimal

x = 10.39 or -0.39

the most logical value for x is 10.39

x = 10.39

t2 = 15/(10.39 - 2) = 1.79

the boat's speed is: 10.39 km/hr

upstream travel time: 1.79 hours

SODIUM SULFATE with ph 7.

   Salts are formed when an acid reacts with a base during a neutralization reaction.

When a strong acid and a weak base reacts, it leads to the formation of acidic salt.When a strong base and weak acid reacts, it leads to the formation of basic salt.When a strong acid and strong base or weak acid and weak base reacts, it leads to the formation of neutral salts.    

Sodium sulfate is a neutral salt, it creates aqueous solutions with a pH of 7. The neutrality of such solutions is due to the fact that Na2SO4 is produced from a strong acid (sulfuric acid) and a strong base (sodium hydroxide).

Hence, a neutral salt and water is formed as products.

  Acidic salts are those which are formed by combination of strong acid and a weak base and have acidic pH of less than 7.

Basic salts are those which are formed by combination of weak acid and a strong base and have basic pH of more than 7.

Neutral salts are those which are formed by combination of weak acid and a weak base or by combination of strong acid and a strong base and have neutral pH equal to 7.

Which of the following events normally activates a GTP-binding protein? a).GTP hydrolysis by the protein Activation of an upstream GTPase-activating protein b).Activation of an upstream guanine nucleotide exchange factor c). Phosphorylation of a bound GDP molecule by an upstream phosphorylase d). Pi release after GTP hydrolysis


B). Activation of an upstream guanine nucleotide exchange factor.  GTP-binding protein that connects cell-surface receptors to Ras protein activation is typically activated by an event.

    Cell-surface receptors and Ras protein activation are principally connected through guanine nucleotide exchange factors (GEFs). By accelerating the separation of GDP from the inactive Ras proteins, they achieve this. Following this, GTP can bind and cause a conformational shift that enables communication with downstream effectors. Rab GTPases and tethering factors start the guanine nucleotide exchange factor in membrane fusion at eukaryotic organelles. The GDP dissociation inhibitor (GDI) chaperone maintains Rabs in their GDP-bound form soluble in the cytoplasm. Rab function depends on guanine nucleotide exchange factors (GEFs), which are present at organelles.

   Ras is activated by alterations in its steady-state equilibrium between its inactive guanosine diphosphate (GDP)-bound and active guanosine triphosphate (GTP)-bound states, which causes an accumulation of Ras-GTP that is mostly momentary. Various target proteins required for crucial cellular activities including division and proliferation are activated by the active ras protein, which functions as a molecular switch. The innate GTPase activity of ras controls the balanced cycling of GTP to GDP, which controls ras-mediated signaling in healthy cells.
