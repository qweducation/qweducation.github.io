11. The Ferrer family drove 750 miles to the beach for summer break. They drove 5 miles per hour faster going to the beach than returning home. If the difference between the number of hours they traveled going to the beach and returning home is 5 hours, what was the average speed when they were on their way to the beach? A. 15 miles per hour B. 25miles per hour C. 30 miles per hour D. 20 miles per hour


Answer:

Let's call the average speed on their way to the beach "s" in miles per hour. Then, the average speed on their way back home would be "s - 5" miles per hour, since they drove 5 miles per hour slower.

To solve for the value of "s", we can use the formula:

distance = speed x time

Let's call the distance from their home to the beach "d" miles. Then, we can write two equations based on the given information:

Going to the beach: d = s x t1

Returning home: d = (s - 5) x t2

where t1 and t2 are the time it took them to travel to the beach and return home, respectively.

We know that the difference between t1 and t2 is 5 hours. That is:

t1 - t2 = 5

We can solve for one of the variables, say t1, in terms of the other variables:

t1 = t2 + 5

Now we can substitute this into the first equation and solve for "s":

d = s x (t2 + 5)

d = s x t2 + 5s

d = (s - 5) x t2 (from the second equation)

t2 = d / (s - 5)

Substituting this expression for t2 into the first equation:

d = s x (d / (s - 5) + 5)

d(s - 5) = s(d + 5d / (s - 5))

d(s - 5) = s((s - 5)d + 5d)

d(s - 5) = sd^2

s^2 - 5s - d = 0

Now we can use the quadratic formula to solve for "s":

s = [5 ± sqrt(5^2 + 4d)] / 2

s = [5 ± sqrt(25 + 4d)] / 2

Since s represents the average speed on their way to the beach, it must be a positive value. Therefore, we take the positive solution:

s = [5 + sqrt(25 + 4d)] / 2

We can substitute the value of d = 750 into this expression and simplify:

s = [5 + sqrt(25 + 4(750))] / 2

s = [5 + sqrt(3025)] / 2

s = (5 + 55) / 2

s = 30

Therefore, the average speed when they were on their way to the beach was 30 miles per hour.

28. what are the intertidal beaches? discuss each beach​


Answer:

On the shore between high and low tide lies the intertidal zone, where land and sea meet. The intertidal zone is underwater during high tide and exposed to air during low tide. The animals and plants that live in this zone must cope with being submerged in water and exposed to the air during different times of the day. Many species of worms, snails, clams, oysters, mussels, and sea stars make the intertidal zone their home. Rocky shores and sandy beaches fall within the intertidal zone. The motion of high tide and low tide creates four zones within the intertidal zone where different animals and plants live.

Spray Zone

The spray zone is the upper part of the beach that occasionally gets splashed but never gets covered by the ocean. This zone is more a part of the land than the ocean. Plants and animals in the spray zone have adapted to living exposed to the air, sun, rain, and even frost.

High Intertidal Zone

The high intertidal zone is flooded during the peaks of the once or twice daily high tides, and out of the water for long stretches of time in between. Here plants and animals are used to living above the water surface.

Mid Intertidal Zone

The middle intertidal zone is generally submerged, except for a period during the turn of low tide. More plants and animals live here because they are not exposed to drying conditions for too long.

Low Intertidal Zone

The lower intertidal zone is exposed to air for only a short period of time at low tide. Life here is adapted to conditions underwater.
