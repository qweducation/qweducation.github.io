Traditional Chinese music has a unique five-note scale, using 1, 2, 3, 5 and 6, while traditional Japanese music uses the "1-3-4-6-7" scale. What is the comparison and differences between music of China to music of Korea?

The music played on the Chinese instrument is generally very fast, technically brilliant, and high in pitch. The Korean instrument, by contrast, is typically played in a low register, with less emphasis on technical brilliance than on the depth and meaning of the music.

Vocal music is one more significant feature of the Korean practice. One of the longest and most extraordinary more established structures is the kagok, which comprises of 26 five-segment solo melodies and one two part harmony. Backups and intervals are given by a little gathering that typically comprises of a kŏmungo (six-stringed zither), a se'piri (a little twofold reed aerophone), a taegŭm (enormous bamboo woodwind), a haegŭm (two-stringed fiddle), and a changgo (hourglass drum). Sijo is a three-line type of traditional Korean verse that might be sung to the sole backup of an hourglass drum; in conventional settings, nonetheless, the drum is typically joined by a twofold reed aerophone, a woodwind, or a fiddle. Story melodies are found in the class called kasa joined by a woodwind and drum.
