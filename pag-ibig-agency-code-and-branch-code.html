3. What agencies are in-charge of implementing the intellectual property code?


The agency in-charge of implementing the Intellectual Property Code or Republic Act No. 8293 is the Intellectual Property Office. It is headed by a Director General who is assisted by two (2) Deputy Director Generals.  The Intellectual Property Office is divided into six (6) bureaus. Each bureau is headed by a Director who is assisted by an Assistant Director. These bureaus are:

Bureau of Patents; Bureau of Trademarks; Bureau of Legal Affairs; Documentation, Information and Technology Transfer Bureau; Management Information System and EDP Bureau; and Administrative, Financial and Personnel Services Bureau.

Functions of the Intellectual Property Office

Examine applications for grant of patent for inventions and register utility models and industrial designs; Examine applications for the registration of marks, geographic indication, and integrated circuits; Register technology transfer arrangements, implement strategies to facilitate technology transfer, and settle disputes involving technology transfer payments; Promote the use of patent information as a tool for technology development; Publish approved patents, marks, utility models, and industrial designs as well as registered technology transfer arrangements regularly in its own publication; Settle contested proceedings affecting intellectual property rights; and Coordinate efforts with other government agencies and the private sector to formulate and implement plans and policies.

A misconduct or violation of the code of ethics may lead to three-fold liability. The consequences of ethical violations vary depending on the type of violation and the governing body. Violations of the Code of Ethics may result in voiding any favorable action obtained by virtue of the violation, restitution requirements, and civil penalties. Violations of the Code of Ethics may also be a violation of local, state, or Federal law. While the Code of Ethics establishes obligations that may be higher than those mandated by law, in any instance where the Code of Ethics and the law conflict, the obligations of the law must take precedence. A breach of duty may occur in tort, and business liability is caused by a breach of duty. In the given example, Ms. D's misconduct of leaking confidential information and compromising details pertaining to a government project, which resulted in other bidders losing the project and it being awarded to a bidder where her sister has financial interest, may lead to legal and ethical consequences.
