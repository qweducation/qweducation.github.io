Answer:

The statement "Temperature causes weathering" is partially correct. Temperature is one of the factors that can contribute to weathering, but it is not the only factor. Weathering is the process by which rocks, soils, and other geological materials are broken down over time. It occurs due to various physical, chemical, and biological processes.

Temperature plays a role in weathering in two primary ways. First, temperature changes can cause rocks to expand and contract, leading to cracks and fractures. This process is called thermal stress, and it can weaken rocks over time, making them more susceptible to other forms of weathering. Second, temperature can affect the rate of chemical reactions that cause weathering. For example, high temperatures can speed up the process of oxidation, which can break down rocks and minerals.

However, other factors such as moisture, wind, and biological activity can also contribute to weathering. Moisture can lead to the erosion of rocks and soils, and the freeze-thaw cycle can cause rocks to crack and split apart. Wind can carry sand and other particles that can abrade rocks, and the roots of plants and other organisms can physically break down rocks as they grow.

In summary, while temperature is an important factor in weathering, it is just one of many factors that contribute to this process. Weathering is a complex phenomenon that involves physical, chemical, and biological processes, and its effects can be seen in the landscapes around us.

21. Science - Assignment 1. What is weathering 2. What are the two types of Weathersing? Define each. 3.A. give 3 examples of mechanical weathering. B. give 3 examples of chemical weathering. 4.give 5 Agent of weathering.​


Answer:

1. Weathering is the process by which rocks, minerals, and soils are broken down and transformed due to exposure to the atmosphere, water, and other natural agents.

2. The two types of weathering are mechanical weathering and chemical weathering.

•Mechanical weathering is the physical disintegration of rocks into smaller fragments without any change in their chemical composition. This type of weathering occurs due to the action of physical forces such as temperature changes, frost wedging, root wedging, and abrasion.

•Chemical weathering is the process of chemical alteration or decomposition of rocks, minerals, and soils due to chemical reactions with water, air, and other substances. This type of weathering occurs due to the action of chemical agents such as acidic rainwater, carbonation, oxidation, and hydration.

A. Examples of mechanical weathering are:

Frost wedging: Water seeps into the cracks of rocks, freezes, and expands, causing the rocks to break apart.

Root wedging: Plant roots grow into cracks of rocks, and as they grow, they exert pressure on the rocks, causing them to break apart.

Abrasion: Rocks are worn down by the scraping action of other rocks or sediments.

B. Examples of chemical weathering are:

Carbonation: Carbon dioxide in the air dissolves in rainwater, forming a weak carbonic acid that reacts with rocks containing calcium carbonate, such as limestone, causing them to dissolve.

Oxidation: Iron-bearing minerals in rocks react with oxygen in the air, causing them to rust and break apart.

Hydration: Minerals in rocks absorb water, causing them to expand and break apart.

The five agents of weathering are:

Water: Water is a powerful agent of weathering, as it can dissolve minerals and carry them away.

Wind: Wind can erode rocks and transport sediment over long distances.

Ice: Ice can cause physical weathering through frost wedging and other processes.

Plants: Plant roots can cause mechanical weathering through root wedging, and plants can also produce acidic substances that cause chemical weathering.

Chemicals: Acidic rainwater and other chemicals can cause chemical weathering of rocks and minerals.
