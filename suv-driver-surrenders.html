4. The bank robbers used an unregistered white SUV as a gateway vehicle. ​


Answer:

Almost every car used in a robbery is stolen. The logic is easy enough to follow: it makes sense to pick a car with no ties to anyone involved in the crime, and to choose one quick enough to get away from the cops (or bland enough to blend in) and of course the right size to transport all the illicit booty.

Even with the above criteria, some of the higher-profile robberies that have gone done have used decidedly pedestrian cars, while other more niche thieves have latched on to unusual and unique rides — making a few exceptions to the “stolen getaway car” rule more than a little mind-blowing.

Let’s take a look at five cars used in real-life robbery getaways, and compare them to the vehicles we would have used in their place.

Ford Fordor Deluxe

Used By: Bonnie Parker and Clyde Barrow

When: Spring of 1934

How Did That Go: Clyde Barrow and Bonnie Parker’s bank-robbing gang had a very successful two-year run behind the wheel of V8-powered Ford sedans, with Barrow apparently even writing to Henry Ford himself in order to praise them for their “sustained speed and freedom from trouble.” That streak of Blue Oval luck would run out on May 23 when the two robbers were ambushed by law enforcement while behind the wheel of a ’34 Ford Fordor Deluxe sedan that had been stolen just a month earlier. Shot into Swiss cheese, the pair did not survive.

What We Would Have Used Instead: While the Fords of the day might have been fast, it’s clear Bonnie and Clyde would have better served on that fateful May day in one of Al Capone’s armoured Cadillacs — although they would likely have been a little harder to steal than the Ford that served as their coffin, which had the keys sitting in the ignition when they found it.

Lotus Carlton

Used By: West Midlands Ram Raiders

When: November 1993 to January 1994

How Did That Go: Stolen from Pershore, Worcestershire, in the U.K., this early-’90s super sedan was implicated in an entire string of ‘ram raids’ in the West Midlands. Named after the robbers’ practice of driving a vehicle directly into a storefront and then cleaning out all the valuables, local police were particularly perturbed by the nearly 400-horsepower Carlton’s ability to completely evade capture. “We simply haven’t been able to get near the thing, and it looks unlikely that we ever will,” the constabulary was quoted as saying.

What We Would Have Used Instead: The thieves were never caught, even out-running a police helicopter on the highway in this hotted-up, turbocharged Vauxhaul design, so we’re not going to argue with their choice of vehicle here.
