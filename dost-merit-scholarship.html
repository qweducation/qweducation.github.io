30. What are literary merits?​


Answer:

Literary merit is subjective, and therefore cannot be defined. There is the universal idea, however, that all novels of literary merit must have advanced diction, appeal to a wide audience, and be considered "timely."

Literary merit is a quality of written work, generally applied to the genre of literary fiction. A work is said to have literary merit (to be a work of art) if it is a work of quality, that is if it has some aesthetic value.

Answer:

Literary merits refer to the artistic and aesthetic qualities of a literary work, such as its style, language, structure, and themes. These are the qualities that make a work of literature significant, valuable, and memorable.

Some common examples of literary merits include:

Originality: A work that is innovative and groundbreaking in its approach to storytelling or the exploration of themes.

Imagery: The use of language and description to create vivid and evocative images that capture the reader's imagination.

Language: The use of language that is poetic, precise, and powerful, with a distinctive style and voice.

Structure: The way a work is organized and structured, whether it's a novel, play, or poem, can contribute to its literary merit.

Characterization: The creation of memorable, complex, and realistic characters that resonate with the reader and help convey the story's themes.

Theme: The exploration of significant and universal themes that are relevant to the human experience, such as love, loss, identity, and social justice.
