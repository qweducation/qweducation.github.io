19. 1. Differentiate the types of validity (Content validity, Construct Validity and Criterion-related Validity) 2. Compare structured and unstructured questionnaire.


Answer:

1. Validity is the extent to which a measure or assessment tool accurately measures what it intends to measure. There are three types of validity:

- Content validity: This refers to the degree to which a measurement tool represents all aspects of the construct being measured. It evaluates the comprehensiveness and representativeness of the items included in the measure. Content validity is often established through expert judgment, and the items are usually based on a careful review of the literature or consultation with subject matter experts.

- Construct validity: This refers to the degree to which a measurement tool accurately measures the theoretical construct it is intended to measure. It involves demonstrating that the measure correlates with other measures or outcomes in a manner consistent with theoretical predictions. This type of validity is usually established through factor analysis, convergent validity, and discriminant validity.

- Criterion-related validity: This refers to the degree to which a measurement tool accurately predicts or correlates with a criterion measure. It involves establishing a correlation between the measure being tested and a criterion measure that is already known to be valid. Criterion-related validity can be established through concurrent validity, which involves comparing the results of the measure being tested with the criterion measure at the same time, or through predictive validity, which involves comparing the results of the measure being tested with the criterion measure at a future point in time.

2. A questionnaire is a research instrument used to collect data from participants in a standardized and structured way. Questionnaires can be structured or unstructured:

- Structured questionnaire: This type of questionnaire has a fixed set of questions with predetermined response options, which are usually closed-ended questions (yes/no, multiple choice, rating scales, etc.). Structured questionnaires are easier to analyze and compare across participants, and they provide more reliable and objective data.

- Unstructured questionnaire: This type of questionnaire is more flexible and open-ended, allowing participants to respond in their own words without predetermined response options. Unstructured questionnaires are often used in qualitative research to explore participants' experiences, perceptions, and attitudes in more depth. They are more subjective and difficult to analyze, but they provide richer and more detailed data.
