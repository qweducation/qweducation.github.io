Vascular plants (from Latin vasculum: duct), also known as Tracheophyta (the tracheophytes /trəˈkiːəfaɪts/, from the Greek trācheia), form a large group of plants (c. 300,000 accepted known species)[5] that are defined as land plants that have lignified tissues (the xylem) for conducting water and minerals throughout the plant. They also have a specialized non-lignified tissue (the phloem) to conduct products of photosynthesis. Vascular plants include the clubmosses, horsetails, ferns, gymnosperms (including conifers) and angiosperms (flowering plants). Scientific names for the group include Tracheophyta,[6][4]:251 Tracheobionta[7] and Equisetopsida sensu lato.
