25. Editorial about Junk foods Topic: No to Junk Foods​


Answer:

As our society becomes more fast-paced and convenience-oriented, the consumption of junk food has become a common habit of many individuals. Junk food is delicious, easily accessible, and affordable, making it an attractive choice for people of all ages. However, the negative effects of consuming junk food on our health cannot be ignored.

Junk food is typically high in calories, sugar, and fat, which can lead to weight gain, obesity, and a host of health problems such as heart disease, diabetes, and high blood pressure. The high levels of sodium and preservatives in junk food can also cause harm to our bodies over time.

Moreover, junk food lacks essential nutrients like vitamins, minerals, and fiber that are necessary for our overall health and well-being. Eating a diet that is high in junk food can lead to malnutrition and a weakened immune system, making us more susceptible to illnesses.

The marketing strategies used by the junk food industry also contribute to the problem of unhealthy eating habits. Advertisements for junk food are often targeted towards children, who are vulnerable to the influence of media and may not yet have developed healthy eating habits.

It is important for individuals to make a conscious effort to reduce their consumption of junk food and opt for healthier alternatives such as fresh fruits, vegetables, and whole grains. Moreover, policymakers and food companies should work together to promote healthier food options and regulate the marketing of junk food

26. Write an opinion article about “Should the government place a tax on junk food and fatty snacks?”


Answer: Maybe yes,

Explanation:  Because with obesity and diabetes at record levels, many public health experts believe governments should tax soda, sweets, junk food, and other unhealthy foods and drinks. Denmark, Finland, France, Hungary, and Mexico have such taxes. So do Berkeley, California and the Navajo Nation. Celebrity chef Jamie Oliver is waging a high-profile campaign to get Britain to tax sugar. Many nutrients and ingredients have been suggested as possible targets for taxes, including fat, saturated fat, salt, artificial sweeteners, and caffeine. Our sense, though, is that only sugar might be a plausible candidate.

Sugar in foods and drinks contributes to obesity, diabetes, and other conditions. By increasing the price of products that contain sugar, taxes can get people to consume less of them and thus improve nutrition and health. Health care costs would be lower, and people would live healthier, longer lives. Governments could put the resulting revenue to good use, perhaps by helping low-income families or cutting other taxes.

That’s the pro case for a sugar tax, and it’s a good one. But policymakers need to consider the downsides too. Taxes impose real costs on consumers who pay the tax or switch to other options that may be more expensive, less enjoyable, or less convenient.
