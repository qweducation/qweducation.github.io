Explanation:

The grammatical technique in which writers show how nouns or pronouns relate to other words in a sentence is through noun cases. The function of a noun within a sentence is referred to as its case. Subjective, objective, and possessive are the three noun cases. Each case refers to the function and placement of a noun in the sentence in relation to the other words.

Subjective

When nouns and pronouns assume the form of the sentence's subject or are employed as predicate nouns, they appear in the subjective (also known as the nominative) case. Predicate nouns are preceded by different forms of the verb "be," which re-identify the subject.

Example:

"The woman is a waitress."

Here, the word "is" -- a "be" verb -- precedes the word "waitress," which renames "[t]he woman." That means the noun case is subjective.

Objective

When a noun or pronoun occurs as a direct or indirect object, the objective case is employed. In sentences, direct objects are more common. They come after transitive or action verbs, whereas indirect objects indicate the verb's action's object as well as the direct object's recipient. It's also appropriate when the noun is the subject of a preposition.

Example:

"The girl jumped over the couch."

Here, the couch is the object of the preposition, making the noun case objective.

Possessive

The possessive case is used to express ownership of nouns and pronouns. The possessive apostrophe is placed to match the ownership indicated by the possessive noun case. While there are exceptions for spelling, the apostrophe comes before the "s" in a singular possessive, while the apostrophe comes after the "s" in plural possessives.

Example:

"Carrie ate Tommy's pancakes."

Tommy is the owner of the pancakes, and so the case is possessive.
