3. list and describe the types of cellular transport


Answer:

There are three main types of cellular transport:

Passive transport: This is the movement of molecules across the cell membrane without requiring any input of energy. Passive transport can be either facilitated or simple diffusion. Facilitated diffusion involves the use of transport proteins to move molecules across the membrane, while simple diffusion does not require any protein assistance.

Active transport: This is the movement of molecules against a concentration gradient, which requires the input of energy. Active transport involves the use of transport proteins to move molecules across the membrane and can be either primary or secondary active transport. Primary active transport directly uses energy from ATP to move molecules, while secondary active transport uses the energy from the concentration gradient of a previously transported substance to move molecules.

Endocytosis: This is the process by which cells take in molecules or particles from the external environment. There are three main types of endocytosis: phagocytosis (ingestion of solid particles), pinocytosis (ingestion of liquids), and receptor-mediated endocytosis (selective uptake of specific molecules).

Overall, these three types of transport allow cells to maintain homeostasis by regulating the movement of molecules in and out of the cell.

Where does a heterotroph obtain its energy?

A. Microorganism

C. Eating other organisms

D. Sun and eating other organisms

Answer: C. Eating other organisms. Heterotrophs are organisms that cannot produce their own food and must obtain energy by consuming other organisms. This is in contrast to autotrophs, which are able to produce their own food through processes such as photosynthesis.

Which of the following occurs in both photosynthesis and cellular respiration with short explanation

A. Production of ATP

B. Conversion of energy from one form to another

C. Movement of molecules across a membrane

D. All of the above

Answer: D. All of the above. Both photosynthesis and cellular respiration involve the production of ATP, the conversion of energy from one form to another, and the movement of molecules across a membrane. In photosynthesis, energy from the sun is converted into chemical energy stored in organic compounds such as glucose, while in cellular respiration, chemical energy stored in organic compounds is converted into ATP, which can be used by cells to power various cellular processes. Both processes also involve the movement of molecules across membranes, as photosynthesis occurs in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplasts and cellular respiration occurs in the mitochondria.
