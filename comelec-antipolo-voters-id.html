Formulate the thesis statement, create an outline using Alphanumeric System and write a summary. Write your answer on a 1 whole yellow pad. A crucial step in nation-building is exercising your right to choose the country’s leaders. However, you can only do that if you are a registered voter. You’re qualified to register as a voter in the Philippines if you meet all the eligibility requirements. You must be a Filipino citizen, at least 18 years old on or before Election Day, a resident of the Philippines for at least 1 year and a resident of the place where you intend to vote for at least 6 months preceding Election Day. On the other hand, you must not have been sentenced by final judgment to suffer imprisonment for at least 1 year, adjudged by final judgment for committing any crime of disloyalty to the government or against national security or declared insane or incompetent by a competent authority. If you are already on the voter’s list, well and good. Just double-check that your record is still active and up to date. Otherwise, follow the steps below to register. First, bring a valid ID to your local COMELEC office, or to satellite registration areas in malls and barangays. Acceptable valid IDs are: Employee ID, with signature of employer or his/her authorized representative, Postal ID, Person with disability (PWD) discount ID, Student’s ID or library card, signed by school authority, Senior Citizen’s ID, Driver’s license, National Bureau of Investigation (NBI) clearance, Passport, SSS/GSIS ID, Integrated Bar of the Philippines (IBP) ID, Professional Regulatory Commission (PRC) license and/or For indigenous Filipinos, a Certificate of Confirmation issued by the National Commission on Indigenous Peoples (NCIP). A cedula (community tax certificate) or police clearance will not be accepted as valid IDs. Next, fill out 3 copies of the form to apply for registration. Get the forms from your local COMELEC office, or download and print the application form from the COMELEC website. Senior citizens, persons with disabilities, and members of indigenous communities will fill out a Supplementary Data Form, and may be assisted by a qualified assistant. Finally, have your biometrics captured. COMELEC personnel will take your photograph, digital signature, and fingerprints. Keep the acknowledgment receipt. It is found at the bottom part of the application form. It will be detached by the COMELEC personnel and given to you. Besides the registration of new applicants, the following voter-related services are also available: Transfer of Voter Registration Record, Reactivation of Voter Registration Record, Change of name due to marriage or court order, Correction of entries in Voter Registration Record, Reinstatement of name in the List of Voters and Inclusion of registration record in the Book of Voters. If you are unsure of the status of your voter registration or biometrics record, contact your local COMELEC office.

25. Voter IDs: Yea or Nay? Explore perspectives and policies related to voter ID laws Then write 1-2 paragraphs describing the benefits and drawbacks of these laws, and your evidence-based recommendations for the policy that would best support free and fair elections.


Explanation:

Voter ID laws require voters to show some form of identification at the polls in order to vote. These laws have been controversial, with proponents arguing that they are necessary to prevent voter fraud and ensure the integrity of elections, and opponents arguing that they disproportionately burden certain groups of voters, such as minorities and low-income individuals, and can effectively disenfranchise them.

There are several arguments in favor of voter ID laws. One argument is that these laws help to prevent in-person voter impersonation, which is when someone casts a vote in the name of another voter. Voter impersonation is a rare occurrence, but it can have serious consequences if it goes undetected. By requiring voters to show ID, officials can verify the identity of the person casting the vote and ensure that the vote is being cast by the person it was intended for.

On the other hand, there are also several arguments against voter ID laws. One argument is that these laws can disproportionately burden certain groups of voters, such as minorities, low-income individuals, and the elderly, who may be less likely to have a government-issued ID or the resources to obtain one. This can effectively disenfranchise these groups, as they may be unable to vote if they do not have the required identification. Additionally, some studies have found that voter ID laws can have a discriminatory impact, with minority and low-income voters being more likely to be disenfranchised by these laws than white and more affluent voters.

In light of these considerations, it is important to strike a balance between ensuring the integrity of elections and protecting the right to vote. One potential policy that could support free and fair elections is providing free voter IDs to individuals who need them, along with voter education efforts to ensure that all eligible voters are aware of the requirements and have the necessary identification. This approach would help to mitigate the potential burden on certain groups of voters while still ensuring the integrity of elections.
