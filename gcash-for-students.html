Solution

Here, $140 is the whole, and we want to find 9.4% of $140. We start by writing the percent as a decimal by moving the decimal point two places to the left (which is equivalent to dividing by 100). We can then compute: tax = 0.094(140) = $13.16 in tax.

EXAMPLE 4

In the news, you hear “tuition is expected to increase by 7% next year.” If tuition this year was $1200 per quarter, what will it be next year?

Solution

The tuition next year will be the current tuition plus an additional 7%, so it will be 107% of this year’s tuition: $1200(1.07) = $1284.

Alternatively, we could have first calculated 7% of $1200: $1200(0.07) = $84.

Notice this is not the expected tuition for next year (we could only wish). Instead, this is the expected increase, so to calculate the expected tuition, we’ll need to add this change to the previous year’s tuition: $1200 + $84 = $1284.

For example, while Nixon was president, it was argued that marijuana was a “gateway” drug, claiming that 80% of marijuana smokers went on to use harder drugs like cocaine. The problem is, this isn’t true. The true claim is that 80% of harder drug users first smoked marijuana. 28. In a class of 6,there are 2students who forgot their lunch. If the teacher chooses 2 students, what is the probability that both of them forgot their lunch?


Answer:

The probability that both of them forgot their lunch is 5.6%.

Further Explanation

The concept of classical probability can be applied to this problem. In Classical probability, the probability for an event to happen is actually the ratio of the possible number of an event to happen and the total possible number of outcomes.

The equation for the classical probability is given by the definition below.

 

                                  [tex]P(E)=\frac{x}{n}[/tex]

                           ; where [tex]x[/tex] is the possible number of events to happen

                                        [tex]n[/tex] is the total possible outcomes

Some Types of Probabilities

1. Classical Probability

2. Conditional Probability

3. Subjective Probability

Two Possible Cases in the Problem

1. The probability of 2 students forgot the lunch is [tex]\frac{2}{6}=\frac{1}{3}[/tex]

2. The probability of 1 student forgot the lunch is [tex]\frac{1}{6}[/tex].

So, the idea here is that if the teacher picks up two students and it happens that the two students forgot their lunch, then the teacher has [tex]\frac{2}{6}=\frac{1}{3}[/tex] probability.

The other possibility is when the teacher picks two students and then one forgot his/her lunch, so the probability is [tex]\frac{1}{6}[/tex].

Since we looking for the probability of these events to happen simultaneously, to answer the problem, multiply the probabilities of each case.  

Solution:

                     [tex]\frac{1}{3}\times{\frac{1}{6}}=\frac{1}{18}=0.056\:\:\text{or}\:\:5.6\%[/tex]

This gives the conclusion that the probability of the teacher picks 2 students and the students forgot their lunch is 5.6%.
